Who Continuum Is For

For people and organizations who want more control over their writing, publishing, and long-term records.

Continuum is for people who want more control over their work than a typical platform account provides.

It is for people who want their writing, publishing workflow, and long-term records to stay under their control.

Continuum runs on your own machine, allowing you to write locally, publish under your own name, and keep your work with you over time.

Writers and independent authors

Writers need more than reach. They need continuity.

A platform may give distribution, but it rarely gives permanence. Policies change, accounts are limited, algorithms shift, links break, and entire services disappear. Continuum is designed for writers who want a publishing workflow that begins with authorship and control rather than platform dependency.

With Continuum, a writer can publish under one name or several, draft and publish notes or long-form articles, and keep their work available over time.

Journalists and researchers

Journalists and researchers often need clear authorship, reliable records, and a body of work that is not entirely dependent on institutional systems or changing third-party platforms.

Continuum supports that by allowing work to be prepared and stored locally, then published when ready. This creates a stronger foundation for attribution and continuity over time.

Human rights activists

Human rights activists often work in environments where communications infrastructure is fragile, centralized platforms are unreliable, and long-term records matter deeply.

Continuum is not a magic shield, but it supports a more resilient publishing model. Work begins under the user’s control and does not depend on a centralized login system. Important records can be kept locally instead of being reduced to a stream of disposable posts.

Ministries and mission-driven organizations

Ministries and foundations often depend on platforms that do not reflect their values and do not provide lasting control over their communications and records.

Continuum offers a different model. It allows organizations to think in terms of stewardship rather than rented access. Messages, articles, and records can be created and kept locally, then published outward when ready.

Developers and builders

Developers can use Continuum as a practical local workspace for writing, publishing, and experimenting with identity and publishing flows.

It is useful not only as an end-user application, but also as a reference point for thinking about local-first architecture and long-term publishing systems.

Independent individuals

Continuum is also for people who may not fit neatly into a category but know they want a different relationship to the digital world.

They want more control over their identity, their work, and how it is published, with less dependence on centralized systems.

What Continuum is not

Continuum is not primarily a hosted platform. It is not a conventional SaaS product. It is not built around account creation, centralized custody, or frictionless dependence.

It is software you run yourself.

That distinction matters. It changes the user’s role from tenant to steward.

In summary

Continuum is for people and organizations who want more control over their writing, publishing, and long-term records.

  • writers
  • journalists
  • researchers
  • human rights activists
  • ministries
  • developers
  • independent individuals

It is for those who want their digital life to rest on something more durable than a login and a feed.
